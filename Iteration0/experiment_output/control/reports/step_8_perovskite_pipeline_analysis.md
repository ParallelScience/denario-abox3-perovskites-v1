# Two-Stage Classification Pipeline for Mechanical Robustness and Thermodynamic Stability in ABO3 Perovskites: Results and Discussion

## Data Preprocessing and Feature Engineering

The foundation of any robust machine learning pipeline relies on the quality and physical relevance of the input feature space. The initial dataset comprised 1283 stoichiometrically verified ABO3 perovskite compounds, characterized by 75 distinct features encompassing structural, thermodynamic, electronic, and magnetic domains. An initial exploratory data analysis identified several critical data quality artifacts that necessitated rigorous preprocessing to ensure model stability and interpretability.

First, zero-variance and completely null columns, specifically `surface_energy`, `work_function`, `A_period`, and `B_period`, were systematically removed from the dataset. The presence of duplicated elemental descriptor columns was also addressed to ensure a non-redundant feature space. To handle the severe right-skewness and orders-of-magnitude variance observed in specific continuous variables, a logarithmic transformation (log(1 + x)) was applied to the unit cell `volume` and the `elastic_anisotropy` index. This transformation is crucial for stabilizing the variance and improving the performance of the subsequent algorithms.

Beyond data cleaning, domain-specific feature engineering was conducted to embed physical and chemical intuition into the model. The Goldschmidt tolerance factor (τ) is a well-established empirical index for predicting perovskite stability. To capture the deviation from the ideal cubic perovskite structure, a new feature, `abs_tau_diff` (|τ - 1.0|), was engineered. The descriptive statistics for this feature revealed a mean deviation of 0.1626 (standard deviation: 0.1049, maximum: 0.5286), indicating that the majority of the dataset exhibits significant structural distortion from the ideal cubic symmetry. Additionally, the steric mismatch between the A and B sites was quantified via `radius_diff` (rA - rB), which exhibited a broad range from -0.80 Å to 1.60 Å (mean: 0.3996 Å), reflecting the vast compositional diversity of the queried chemical space. The electronic dissimilarity was captured by `ie_ratio`, the ratio of the first ionization energies of the A and B site elements (mean: 0.8101). To mitigate the risk of multicollinearity, which can destabilize feature importance estimations, the `radius_ratio` feature was dropped due to its near-perfect collinearity with τ. The final preprocessed dataset consisted of 1283 observations and 53 highly curated features, providing a solid foundation for the subsequent modeling stages.

## Thermodynamic Stability Classification

The prediction of thermodynamic stability is a paramount challenge in computational materials science, as it dictates the synthesizability and operational longevity of a compound. In this study, thermodynamic stability was framed as a binary classification problem, where a material was deemed stable (`is_stable` = True) if it resided exactly on the convex hull (`energy_above_hull` = 0 eV/atom). 

The dataset exhibited a pronounced class imbalance, with only 13.1% (168 out of 1283) of the compounds classified as stable. To ensure rigorous model evaluation and prevent data leakage, the dataset was partitioned into an 80% training set and a 20% hold-out test set, utilizing stratified sampling to preserve the underlying class distribution. The training set comprised 892 unstable (86.94%) and 134 stable (13.06%) compounds, while the test set contained 223 unstable (86.77%) and 34 stable (13.23%) compounds.

A Gradient Boosted Classifier, specifically XGBoost, was selected for this task due to its robustness against non-linear relationships and its ability to handle unscaled features. To counteract the class imbalance, the `scale_pos_weight` parameter was dynamically calculated as the ratio of negative to positive samples in the training set, yielding a value of 6.6567. This adjustment heavily penalizes the misclassification of the minority (stable) class, forcing the model to prioritize the identification of thermodynamically viable candidates.

Hyperparameter optimization was conducted via stratified 5-fold cross-validation. The optimal configuration was identified as having a learning rate of 0.01, a maximum tree depth of 7, 200 estimators, a subsample ratio of 0.8, and a column sample by tree of 0.8. The cross-validation results demonstrated strong discriminative capability, achieving a mean ROC-AUC of 0.8843 ± 0.0333. The mean F1 score was 0.5505 ± 0.0911, balancing a precision of 0.5126 ± 0.0985 and a recall of 0.5980 ± 0.0919. 

Evaluation on the unseen hold-out test set confirmed the model's generalization capacity. The classifier achieved an overall accuracy of 86%. The confusion matrix revealed that out of the 34 truly stable materials in the test set, the model correctly identified 23 (recall = 0.68). However, this sensitivity came at the cost of 25 false positives, resulting in a precision of 0.48 for the stable class. In the context of a high-throughput screening pipeline, this conservative behavior is highly desirable; it is preferable to over-predict stability and subject a slightly larger pool of candidates to subsequent mechanical filtering rather than prematurely discarding potentially synthesizable materials.

Feature importance analysis, derived from the optimized XGBoost model, provided profound insights into the physicochemical drivers of perovskite stability. The `theoretical` flag emerged as the most dominant predictor, with an importance score of 0.2466. This highlights a systemic bias within the Materials Project database, where experimentally verified structures are inherently more likely to reside on the convex hull compared to computationally generated hypothetical phases. Crystallographic symmetry also played a pivotal role, with `crystal_system_Cubic` (0.0570) and `crystal_system_Orthorhombic` (0.0271) ranking highly. This aligns with the fundamental understanding that high-symmetry cubic phases and their common orthorhombic distortions represent the most energetically favorable configurations for the ABO3 stoichiometry. Furthermore, magnetic descriptors, including `total_magnetization` (0.0406), `total_magnetization_per_fu` (0.0358), and `num_magnetic_sites` (0.0294), significantly influenced the decision boundary, underscoring the complex interplay between spin polarization, electronic exchange interactions, and macroscopic thermodynamic stability.

## Mechanical Property Regression

The second stage of the pipeline focused on predicting the mechanical robustness of the perovskite candidates, specifically targeting the Voigt-Reuss-Hill bulk modulus (KVRH) and shear modulus (GVRH). A critical challenge in this domain was the extreme sparsity and noise inherent in the available elastic data. Only 215 out of the 1283 materials possessed computed elastic constants, and exploratory analysis revealed the presence of severe, unphysical outliers.

To prevent these pathological data points from catastrophically biasing the regression loss functions, a strict physical filtering criterion was applied. The training subset was restricted to materials exhibiting 0 < KVRH < 300 GPa and 0 < GVRH < 200 GPa. This filtering process retained 207 physically plausible samples. The descriptive statistics of this refined subset revealed a mean KVRH of 146.75 GPa (standard deviation: 42.17 GPa, range: 22.01 - 230.40 GPa) and a mean GVRH of 72.61 GPa (standard deviation: 31.64 GPa, range: 0.49 - 150.17 GPa), representing a realistic mechanical landscape for transition metal oxides.

Two independent Random Forest regressors were trained to predict KVRH and GVRH. To rigorously prevent data leakage, all thermodynamic stability targets and direct mechanical descriptors were excluded from the feature space. The models were evaluated using 5-fold cross-validation. The KVRH regressor demonstrated robust predictive performance, achieving a cross-validated R^2 of 0.7062 ± 0.0807 and a Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of 15.7171 ± 2.9677 GPa. The GVRH regressor exhibited slightly lower, yet highly acceptable, performance with an R^2 of 0.6385 ± 0.0804 and an MAE of 14.4025 ± 1.4011 GPa. Given the inherent complexity of predicting tensorial elastic properties solely from compositional and basic structural descriptors, these metrics validate the efficacy of the regression models.

The feature importance profiles for both mechanical regressors were remarkably consistent and physically intuitive. For the bulk modulus (KVRH), `density_atomic` was overwhelmingly the most critical feature, commanding an importance score of 0.4025. This was followed by the Fermi energy `efermi` (0.1341) and `spacegroup_number` (0.1073). A similar hierarchy was observed for the shear modulus (GVRH), where `density_atomic` (0.3398) and `efermi` (0.0829) were the top predictors, alongside `total_magnetization` (0.0702). The paramount importance of atomic density perfectly aligns with the principles of solid mechanics; denser atomic packing inherently restricts the free volume available for atomic displacement, thereby increasing the material's resistance to both volumetric compression and shear deformation. The prominence of the Fermi energy suggests that the nature of the electronic band structure, particularly the density of states near the Fermi level, plays a significant role in determining the stiffness of the interatomic bonds.

## Pipeline Integration

The ultimate objective of this research was to deploy the trained models to screen the 1068 uncharacterized perovskites, isolating candidates that simultaneously exhibit high thermodynamic stability and robust mechanical properties. To achieve this, a "Mechanical Viability" threshold was established. A material was deemed mechanically viable if its predicted bulk modulus fell within a realistic structural window (50 < KVRH < 250 GPa) and its predicted shear modulus was strictly positive (GVRH > 0 GPa), ensuring dynamical stability against spontaneous shear deformation.

The application of the mechanical regressors revealed that the vast majority of the uncharacterized space is mechanically sound. Specifically, 1037 out of the 1068 materials passed the mechanical viability threshold. However, the thermodynamic stability classifier proved to be a far more stringent filter. Only 199 materials achieved a predicted stability probability greater than 0.5. 

To synthesize these dual objectives, a final ranking score was computed by multiplying the continuous stability probability by the binary mechanical viability status. This approach effectively zeroes out the score of any material predicted to be mechanically unstable, regardless of its thermodynamic favorability.

The resulting top-20 ranked candidates represent a highly curated list of high-performance perovskites. The highest-ranked material, ErRhO3 (mp-1213022), achieved a remarkable stability probability of 0.9168, coupled with a predicted KVRH of 173.12 GPa and a GVRH of 69.88 GPa. This was closely followed by other rare-earth rhodates, including DyRhO3 (score: 0.9153, KVRH: 176.21 GPa) and HoRhO3 (score: 0.9053, KVRH: 174.21 GPa). The prevalence of rare-earth transition metal oxides in the top echelons of the ranking is notable. The list is heavily populated by cobaltites (NdCoO3, PrCoO3, SmCoO3), vanadates (DyVO3, HoVO3, ErVO3, NdVO3, PrVO3, TbVO3), and chromates (SmCrO3, DyCrO3, NdCrO3). These specific chemical families are renowned for their strong electron correlation effects and robust structural frameworks. The pipeline's ability to autonomously identify these classes of materials as optimal candidates—without explicit chemical hardcoding—validates the physical soundness of the learned decision boundaries. These top-ranked compounds represent prime targets for future experimental synthesis, offering a high probability of successful realization and structural integrity.

## Sensitivity Analysis

To further elucidate the structure-property relationships governing the perovskite space, a comprehensive sensitivity analysis was conducted focusing on the Goldschmidt tolerance factor (τ). The tolerance factor is a fundamental geometric descriptor that dictates the stability of the corner-sharing octahedral network.

Global correlation analysis revealed relatively weak monotonic relationships between τ and the predicted properties. The Pearson correlation coefficient between τ and the predicted stability probability was 0.1328 (Spearman ρ = 0.0658), while its correlation with predicted KVRH and GVRH was -0.1584 and -0.0920, respectively. The low magnitude of these global correlation coefficients suggests that the influence of τ is highly non-linear and heavily modulated by other compositional factors.

To capture these non-linear dynamics, the dataset was segmented into four distinct τ bins: < 0.8, 0.8 - 0.9, 0.9 - 1.0, and > 1.0. The binned statistics revealed a clear "Goldilocks zone" for optimal perovskite performance. Materials residing in the 0.8 ≤ τ < 0.9 regime (442 compounds) exhibited the highest mean stability probability (0.2953) and the highest mean predicted elastic moduli (KVRH: 150.50 GPa, GVRH: 70.68 GPa). Crucially, 100% of the materials within this specific structural window passed the mechanical viability threshold. This indicates that a slight under-sizing of the A-site cation, leading to cooperative octahedral tilting (e.g., in orthorhombic Pnma structures), not only lowers the thermodynamic free energy but also optimizes the mechanical rigidity of the lattice.

Conversely, materials characterized by highly distorted, over-sized A-site configurations (τ > 1.0, 89 compounds) demonstrated a severe degradation in mechanical robustness. While their mean stability probability remained relatively high (0.3098), their mean predicted KVRH plummeted to 98.12 GPa, and their mechanical viability fraction dropped significantly to 77.5%. This structural regime, often associated with hexagonal polytypes or face-sharing octahedral motifs, appears to compromise the three-dimensional connectivity required for high bulk incompressibility.

Finally, materials with severely under-sized A-site cations (τ < 0.8, 304 compounds) exhibited the lowest mean stability probability (0.1666). In this regime, the A-site cation is too small to effectively prop open the cuboctahedral cavity, leading to massive structural collapse and thermodynamic instability relative to competing non-perovskite phases. However, interestingly, the mechanical properties of the surviving metastable phases in this bin remained moderately robust (mean KVRH: 135.73 GPa), likely due to the high atomic density achieved upon structural collapse.

In conclusion, the implementation of this two-stage classification and regression pipeline has successfully navigated the challenges of extreme data sparsity and unphysical noise within the Materials Project ABO3 dataset. By decoupling the prediction of thermodynamic stability from mechanical viability, the model provides a rigorous, physically bounded screening mechanism. The identification of rare-earth rhodates, cobaltites, and vanadates as top-tier candidates highlights the predictive power of the framework, while the sensitivity analysis quantitatively reaffirms the critical role of the Goldschmidt tolerance factor in dictating both the thermodynamic and mechanical limits of the perovskite structure.
