The current research has successfully utilized global structural descriptors (τ, μ, volume) to predict stability and mechanical properties. However, these descriptors are "mean-field" approximations that ignore the local coordination environment and the specific electronic nature of the B-site transition metal d-orbitals, which are the primary drivers of Jahn-Teller distortions and octahedral tilting in perovskites. 

I hypothesize that the performance plateau in mechanical property regression (specifically G_VRH) is due to the loss of information regarding the local symmetry breaking. By engineering "Local Symmetry Descriptors"—specifically calculating the variance of the B-O bond lengths (if available via structural data) or, more feasibly within the current dataset, the "Octahedral Distortion Index" derived from the `spacegroup_number` and `crystal_system`—we can better capture the non-linear coupling between electronic configuration and mechanical shear resistance. 

Furthermore, I propose a "Multi-Fidelity Transfer Learning" approach: instead of treating the 1068 uncharacterized materials as a single block, we should use the 215 elastic-labeled samples to train a "Physics-Informed Neural Network" (PINN) that incorporates the Pugh ratio (G/K) as a physical constraint in the loss function. This will force the model to learn the relationship between bulk modulus and shear modulus as a coupled system, rather than independent regressions, thereby improving the prediction of the Pugh ratio for the uncharacterized set and identifying materials with high shear-rigidity (high G_VRH) that are currently being missed by independent regression models.
