# Results and Discussion

## 1. Thermodynamic Stability Classification

The foundational stage of our sequential pipeline involved the classification of thermodynamic stability across the full 1283-compound ABO3 perovskite dataset. Recognizing the inherent inaccuracies of Density Functional Theory (DFT) formation energies—typically on the order of $\pm 50$ meV/atom—and the stabilizing effect of configurational entropy at finite temperatures, we defined a "soft" stability threshold. Materials with an energy above the convex hull ($E_{hull}$) of less than 0.05 eV/atom were classified as stable or synthetically accessible metastable phases. This thresholding yielded a relatively balanced target distribution, with 42.6% of the dataset classified as stable and 57.4% as highly metastable or dynamically unstable.

The Gradient Boosting Classifier (GBC) trained on structural and compositional descriptors demonstrated robust predictive capabilities, achieving an Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (AUC-ROC) of 0.8798 and an Average Precision (PR-AUC) of 0.8122 (as depicted in <code>step_2_roc_curve_1_1776236389.png</code> and <code>step_2_pr_curve_2_1776236390.png</code>). The confusion matrix further elucidates the model's performance: out of 546 truly stable compounds, the model correctly identified 459 (Recall = 0.84), while maintaining a precision of 0.74. The 164 false positives—materials predicted to be stable but possessing $E_{hull} \ge 0.05$ eV/atom—are of particular interest. In materials informatics, such false positives often represent polymorphs that are dynamically stable and potentially synthesizable under non-equilibrium conditions (e.g., via epitaxial strain or rapid quenching), suggesting that the model has learned the underlying structural heuristics of the perovskite phase rather than merely memorizing the DFT convex hull.

Feature importance analysis revealed that the model relies heavily on a combination of crystallographic symmetry and geometric packing. The total number of atomic sites in the unit cell (`nsites`, 18.5% importance) and the space group number (14.6%) were the dominant features, reflecting the fact that highly distorted, low-symmetry supercells are frequently associated with structural instability or complex magnetic orderings that deviate from the ideal cubic perovskite prototype.

Crucially, the Goldschmidt tolerance factor ($\tau$) emerged as the most important purely compositional descriptor (7.9% importance). Physically, $\tau$ quantifies the geometric mismatch between the A-site and B-site cations within the corner-sharing oxygen octahedra network. Values of $\tau$ approaching 1.0 indicate an ideal fit for the cubic $Pm\bar{3}m$ structure. As $\tau$ deviates from this ideal range (typically below 0.85 or above 1.05), the structure must accommodate the strain through cooperative octahedral tilting (e.g., Glazer tilt systems), leading to lower-symmetry orthorhombic or rhombohedral phases, or ultimately destabilizing the perovskite lattice entirely in favor of non-perovskite polymorphs. The model's strong reliance on $\tau$, alongside the `volume_residual` (7.3%)—which captures the deviation of the DFT-relaxed volume from a purely geometric hard-sphere estimate—demonstrates that the GBC successfully internalized the fundamental physical chemistry governing perovskite phase stability.

## 2. Mechanical Property Regression and Uncertainty Quantification

The second stage of the pipeline addressed the prediction of mechanical robustness, a task severely complicated by extreme data sparsity and the presence of unphysical artifacts. Exploratory data analysis of the 215-sample elastic subset revealed catastrophic outliers, such as BaRuO3 exhibiting a computed Voigt-Reuss-Hill bulk modulus ($K_{VRH}$) exceeding $10^6$ GPa—orders of magnitude stiffer than diamond. Such anomalies typically arise from convergence failures or pathological potential energy surfaces during the DFT strain-stress tensor calculations. To prevent these outliers from dominating the mean squared error loss landscape, we applied strict, physically motivated bounds, filtering the dataset to $0 < K_{VRH} \le 300$ GPa and $0 \le G_{VRH} \le 200$ GPa. This rigorous curation retained 207 reliable samples for model training.

Gaussian Process Regressors (GPR), utilizing a composite Radial Basis Function (RBF) and White Noise kernel, were trained on the standardized feature space. The GPR model for the bulk modulus achieved a cross-validated $R^2$ of 0.7369 and a Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) of 21.1356 GPa. The predictive performance for the shear modulus ($G_{VRH}$) was comparatively lower, with an $R^2$ of 0.5610 and an RMSE of 20.2574 GPa. This discrepancy is physically consistent: the bulk modulus represents the isotropic resistance to volume change and correlates strongly with scalar properties like atomic density and volume, whereas the shear modulus dictates resistance to shape change and is highly sensitive to the directionality of covalent bonds and specific angular distortions of the $BO_6$ octahedra, which are inherently more difficult to capture using isotropic compositional descriptors.

The parity plots (<code>step_5_gpr_parity_1776236871.png</code>) illustrate that while the models capture the macroscopic trends effectively, predictions at the extreme upper bounds of the moduli spectrum exhibit higher variance. However, the true value of the GPR framework lies in its non-parametric uncertainty quantification. The observed RMSE values of ~20 GPa are remarkably close to the intrinsic epistemic uncertainty of the underlying DFT-PBE methodology, which typically exhibits errors of 10-20% when compared to experimental ultrasonic or Brillouin scattering measurements of elastic constants. Consequently, the posterior variance output by the GPR provides a highly reliable, localized confidence interval for each prediction, effectively encapsulating both the sparsity of the training manifold and the aleatoric noise of the ab initio data.

## 3. Applicability Domain and Active Learning Candidate Selection

A fundamental pitfall in materials machine learning is the blind extrapolation of models trained on sparse datasets to vast, uncharacterized chemical spaces. To ensure the reliability of our mechanical predictions for the 1068 uncharacterized perovskites, we implemented a One-Class Support Vector Machine (SVM) to delineate the "Applicability Domain" (AD) of the elastic training set.

The AD classification revealed that 634 materials (59.4%) fell "In-Domain," while 434 materials (40.6%) were flagged as "Out-of-Domain" (High-Uncertainty). The Principal Component Analysis (PCA) projection (<code>step_5_domain_pca_1776236871.png</code>) provides a compelling visual validation of this approach. The characterized training samples occupy a distinct, dense sub-region of the first two principal components. The In-Domain uncharacterized materials successfully map onto this established manifold, indicating that their predicted mechanical properties interpolate within known chemical heuristics. Conversely, the Out-of-Domain materials are scattered across peripheral regions of the PCA space, representing novel elemental combinations or extreme structural distortions absent from the training data.

This domain-aware stratification has profound implications for future computational workflows. Predictions for the 634 In-Domain materials can be utilized immediately for experimental screening with a high degree of confidence. However, the 434 Out-of-Domain materials represent the frontier of the perovskite chemical space. To systematically explore this frontier, we formulated an active learning acquisition function based on the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB). This function balances exploitation (the GBC-predicted probability of thermodynamic stability) with exploration (the normalized GPR predictive uncertainty for the elastic moduli).

The distribution of UCB scores (<code>step_5_ucb_distribution_1776236871.png</code>) allowed us to rank the uncharacterized materials. The top 50 candidates—including compounds like KAsO3, various polymorphs of NaNbO3, and BaPtO3—are predominantly Out-of-Domain materials that are highly likely to be stable but possess massive mechanical uncertainty. These specific compounds are the most valuable targets for future high-throughput DFT elastic tensor calculations. Resolving their mechanical properties will provide the maximum possible information gain, rapidly expanding the Applicability Domain and improving the global accuracy of subsequent model iterations.

## 4. Electronic Profiling and Multi-Objective Optimization

To construct a holistic profile of the viable perovskite space, we augmented the thermodynamic and mechanical predictions with electronic property models. Given the bimodal nature of the dataset (48.6% metallic), we employed a two-stage hurdle architecture. The Random Forest classifier for metallicity (`is_metal`) achieved exceptional performance, with an ROC-AUC of 0.9113 and a PR-AUC of 0.9099 (<code>step_5_is_metal_roc_1776236871.png</code>, <code>step_5_is_metal_pr_1776236871.png</code>). For the non-metallic subset, a subsequent Random Forest regressor predicted the PBE band gap with an $R^2$ of 0.6918 and an RMSE of 0.7988 eV. While PBE systematically underestimates absolute band gaps due to self-interaction errors, the model successfully captures the relative optoelectronic trends across the compositional space.

Furthermore, we derived the Pugh ratio ($G/K$)—a critical empirical metric where values $>0.57$ typically indicate brittleness and $<0.57$ indicate ductility. Rather than simply dividing the mean GPR predictions, we employed Monte Carlo sampling of the GPR posterior distributions to propagate the uncertainty rigorously. This ensures that the reported Pugh ratios for the In-Domain materials are statistically sound and reflect the coupled variance of the bulk and shear moduli.

The culmination of this pipeline is the multi-objective optimization space (<code>step_5_pareto_frontier_1776236871.png</code>), restricted strictly to the 634 In-Domain materials to guarantee physical reliability. We sought to simultaneously maximize the predicted probability of stability, the shear modulus ($G_{VRH}$, as a proxy for structural rigidity), and the band gap (targeting robust dielectric or optoelectronic substrates). The Pareto frontier analysis identified exactly 11 optimal candidates that represent the absolute best trade-offs among these three competing properties.

The chemical diversity of these 11 Pareto-optimal candidates is highly illuminating, comprising 8 unique A-site elements (Nd, Sm, Ce, Dy, Tb, Ca, Sr) but only 2 unique B-site elements: Aluminum (Al) and Silicon (Si). The dominance of Al and Si on the B-site is deeply rooted in their physical chemistry. Both elements form highly covalent, directional bonds with oxygen, resulting in exceptionally rigid $BO_6$ octahedral networks that drive up the shear modulus. Furthermore, the large electronegativity difference between these B-site cations and oxygen leads to deep valence bands and high conduction band minima, naturally yielding wide band gaps.

The Pareto frontier is distinctly bifurcated into two chemical families. The first comprises rare-earth aluminates, such as NdAlO3 (Stability Probability = 0.9442, $G_{VRH}$ = 95.4 GPa, Band Gap = 4.09 eV) and SmAlO3 (Stability Probability = 0.9219, $G_{VRH}$ = 99.6 GPa, Band Gap = 4.13 eV). These materials offer an outstanding balance: they are almost certainly thermodynamically stable, possess high mechanical stiffness, and are wide-band-gap insulators. They represent prime candidates for immediate experimental synthesis, with potential applications as high-k dielectric materials, microwave resonators, or robust structural ceramics.

The second family consists of alkaline-earth silicates, notably CaSiO3 and SrSiO3. These compounds exhibit the highest predicted shear moduli on the frontier (ranging from 111 to 130 GPa) and moderate band gaps (2.6 to 3.8 eV). However, their predicted stability probabilities are significantly lower (0.08 to 0.29). This is a physically accurate reflection of silicate perovskite thermodynamics: at ambient pressures, the ideal cubic perovskite phase of CaSiO3 is metastable relative to other polymorphs (such as wollastonite). Silicate perovskites typically require extreme pressures to stabilize—such as Bridgmanite, the most abundant mineral in the Earth's lower mantle. The model's ability to identify these silicates as mechanically supreme yet thermodynamically marginal at ambient conditions underscores the physical validity of the learned feature space.

## 5. Conclusion

In summary, this research successfully implemented a domain-constrained, multi-objective machine learning pipeline to navigate the complex property space of ABO3 perovskites. By rigorously filtering unphysical DFT artifacts and employing Gaussian Process Regression, we established a reliable mechanical property predictor with intrinsic uncertainty quantification. The integration of a One-Class SVM Applicability Domain ensures that predictions are not blindly extrapolated, safeguarding downstream experimental efforts. The identification of 11 Pareto-optimal candidates—specifically rare-earth aluminates and alkaline-earth silicates—provides highly targeted recommendations for the discovery of mechanically robust, stable, and electronically functional perovskite materials. Furthermore, the active learning framework provides a mathematically rigorous roadmap for future high-throughput computational campaigns, ensuring that expensive DFT resources are allocated to the regions of chemical space that will yield the greatest improvement in predictive modeling.
