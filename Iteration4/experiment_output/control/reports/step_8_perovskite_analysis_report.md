# Results

## Data Engineering

The initial phase of the analytical pipeline involved rigorous data cleaning and feature engineering to prepare the ABO3 perovskite dataset for downstream machine learning tasks. The raw dataset was systematically pruned of uninformative and completely missing columns, specifically `surface_energy`, `work_function`, `A_period`, and `B_period`. Furthermore, the `radius_ratio` feature was removed to mitigate severe multicollinearity with the Goldschmidt tolerance factor ($	au$), and duplicate columns resulting from data retrieval artifacts were excised. The resulting cleaned dataset comprises 1283 stoichiometrically verified observations across 54 features.

To capture the physical and geometric nuances of the perovskite structure, several domain-specific features were engineered. The Valence Electron Count (VEC) was calculated for each compound, serving as a fundamental descriptor of the electronic structure that dictates the filling of bonding and antibonding states. The VEC exhibited a mean of 28.70 ± 4.80, ranging from 22 to 46. A `volume_residual` feature was computed as the difference between the DFT-relaxed volume and a geometric estimate based on ionic radii. This feature acts as a macroscopic indicator of internal strain, yielding a mean of 136.24 ± 282.82 Å³. Additionally, a `tilt_proxy` was derived to quantify structural distortions, defined as the ratio of the actual volume to an idealized cubic cell volume calculated from $	au$. This proxy provides a continuous measure of symmetry breaking, with a mean of 1.086 ± 0.782. To address the heavy right-skewness observed during exploratory data analysis, log-transformations ($\log(1+x)$) were applied to the `volume` and `elastic_anisotropy` features. The transformed `volume` exhibited a mean of 5.057 ± 0.814, while the transformed `elastic_anisotropy` (available for the 215-sample elastic subset) had a mean of 0.717 ± 1.139.

## Stability Classification

Thermodynamic stability was modeled as a binary classification task, defining stable compounds as those residing strictly on the zero-temperature DFT convex hull (`energy_above_hull` = 0). A Gradient Boosting Classifier was trained using a comprehensive suite of structural and compositional features. Evaluated via 5-fold stratified cross-validation, the model achieved an ROC-AUC of 0.7827 ± 0.0514, indicating a strong discriminative ability to rank stable compounds higher than metastable ones. The F1 score was 0.3249 ± 0.1000, with a precision of 0.5456 ± 0.1636 and a recall of 0.2324 ± 0.0743. The relatively low recall reflects the inherent difficulty in identifying the sparse stable minority class (comprising only 13.1% of the dataset) amidst a vast, complex space of metastable configurations.

When applied to the full dataset, the model identified 80 True Positives and 1109 True Negatives, while producing 88 False Negatives and 6 False Positives. A decomposition pathway analysis was conducted on the False Positives to understand the physical nature of their misclassification. The top False Positives—LuVO3, EuVO3, EuCrO3, EuNiO3, EuMnO3, and ErNiO3—exhibit high predicted stability probabilities (e.g., 0.653 for LuVO3) despite having non-zero energies above the hull (e.g., 0.0058 eV/atom for LuVO3). These compounds are likely structurally metastable; they represent viable perovskite polymorphs that are only marginally outcompeted by non-perovskite ground states. This suggests that the model is identifying a broader "synthesizability window" rather than strictly adhering to the 0 K DFT ground state, capturing the underlying chemical compatibility of the ABO3 stoichiometry. The distribution of equilibrium reaction energies for these classifications is visualized in <code>data/step_2_eq_reaction_energy_dist_2_20260415_085651.png</code>.

## Mechanical Viability

To bypass the unphysical noise and extreme sparsity inherent in the elastic dataset, a "Mechanical Viability" classifier was developed. The raw elastic data in the Materials Project dataset for these perovskites contains severe anomalies, such as bulk moduli exceeding $10^6$ GPa or highly negative shear moduli. Attempting to regress these continuous values would lead to catastrophic failure of standard loss functions. Therefore, the 215-sample elastic subset was filtered to exclude extreme outliers, retaining entries where $-20 < G_{VRH} < 200$ GPa and $0 < K_{VRH} < 300$ GPa. This adjusted filter yielded a subset of 210 samples, comprising 207 mechanically robust configurations ($G_{VRH} > 0$) and 3 dynamically unstable configurations ($G_{VRH} \le 0$).

A Gradient Boosting Classifier was trained to distinguish between these classes. Due to the extreme class imbalance, a 3-fold stratified cross-validation was employed, achieving an accuracy of 0.9905 ± 0.0067, an F1 score of 0.9952 ± 0.0034, and an ROC-AUC of 0.8333 ± 0.2357. While these near-perfect metrics must be interpreted with caution given the scarcity of the minority class, permutation feature importance analysis confirmed that the model learned physically meaningful relationships. The model relied heavily on structural descriptors, particularly the engineered `volume_residual` and `tilt_proxy`, rather than memorizing noise. The feature importance rankings, complete with error bars, are detailed in <code>data/step_3_feature_importance_3_20260415_090018.png</code>. By framing mechanical robustness as a binary classification, the pipeline successfully isolates physically sound candidates without being biased by severe numerical artifacts.

## The Silicate Paradox and Pressure-Volume Analysis

A critical investigation into the relationship between structural descriptors and thermodynamic stability revealed a distinct behavioral divergence between silicate and non-silicate perovskites, herein termed the "Silicate Paradox." The dataset contains 81 silicate-based perovskites and 1202 non-silicate perovskites.

Correlation analysis between the `volume_residual` and `energy_above_hull` demonstrated a significant negative correlation for silicates (Pearson $r = -0.4888$, $p = 3.66 \times 10^{-6}$). This implies that as the silicate structure is compressed (yielding a more negative volume residual), it becomes increasingly unstable relative to the convex hull. This reflects the massive internal strain and the energetic penalty of forcing silicon—which strongly prefers tetrahedral coordination—into an octahedral B-site geometry, a state typically only accessible under extreme planetary mantle pressures.

In stark contrast, non-silicates exhibited a positive correlation (Pearson $r = 0.1689$, $p = 3.80 \times 10^{-9}$). For these 1202 compounds, overly expanded volumes (positive volume residuals) correlate with instability, suggesting that under-bonded or highly porous metastable frameworks are energetically penalized. The correlation between `volume_residual` and the model's predicted stability probability further supported this dichotomy, showing a statistically significant relationship for non-silicates ($r = 0.0627$, $p = 0.0296$) but not for silicates. These divergent trends underscore the necessity of domain-aware feature engineering, as a global linear model would fail to capture these opposing physical regimes. The pressure-volume relationships are visually captured in the scatter plots provided in <code>data/step_4_silicate_paradox_4_20260415_090226.png</code>.

## Electronic Properties

To map the optoelectronic property space, a two-stage hurdle model was implemented, addressing the zero-inflated nature of the band gap distribution. The dataset is nearly evenly split, with 624 metals (`band_gap` = 0.0 eV) and 659 non-metals.

In the first stage, a Random Forest Classifier with balanced class weights was trained to predict metallicity (`is_metal`). In the second stage, a Random Forest Regressor was trained exclusively on the 659 non-metallic compounds to predict the continuous band gap magnitude. Evaluated via 5-fold cross-validation, the regressor achieved a Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) of 0.8166 eV and a Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of 0.5470 eV. While this error margin is non-negligible, it must be contextualized within the known limitations of the PBE functional, which systematically underestimates band gaps, particularly for strongly correlated d-electron systems common in transition metal perovskites. The hurdle architecture effectively prevents the model from predicting unphysical negative band gaps or struggling with the massive point mass at zero. The performance of both the classifier (via confusion matrix) and the regressor (via predicted vs. actual scatter plot) is illustrated in <code>data/step_5_electronic_hurdle_5_20260415_090342.png</code>.

## Applicability Domain

Machine learning models in materials science are highly susceptible to catastrophic extrapolation when applied to chemical spaces outside their training distribution. To ensure the reliability of predictions for the 1068 uncharacterized materials, an Applicability Domain (AD) was defined using a One-Class Support Vector Machine (SVM) trained on the scaled feature space of the 210-sample elastic subset.

The AD analysis revealed that out of the 1068 uncharacterized materials, 695 fall within the Applicability Domain (In-Domain), while 373 reside outside it (Out-of-Domain). This finding is a critical safety mechanism, highlighting that nearly a third of the uncharacterized space represents structural or chemical motifs not adequately represented in the elastic training subset. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was utilized to project the multidimensional feature space into two dimensions. The Euclidean distance to the nearest training sample in the PCA-transformed space was computed for each uncharacterized material, providing a continuous metric of extrapolation risk. The distribution of the training set, In-Domain, and Out-of-Domain uncharacterized materials is visualized in <code>data/step_6_applicability_domain_6_20260415_090509.png</code>.

## Candidate Selection

The final stage of the pipeline synthesized the predictive models to isolate high-performance, mechanically robust, and thermodynamically stable perovskite candidates from the uncharacterized pool. A composite ranking score was formulated by aggregating the ranks of three criteria: (1) high stability probability, (2) high mechanical viability probability, and (3) minimal PCA distance to the Applicability Domain boundary.

To focus on functional insulators and semiconductors, the 1068 candidates were filtered to exclude any materials predicted to be metallic, leaving 531 non-metallic candidates. A diversity filter was then applied by clustering the candidates by their chemical system (`chemsys`) and retaining only the top candidate from each cluster. This step is crucial to prevent the final list from being dominated by trivial compositional variations of a single highly ranked prototype, resulting in 240 unique, diverse candidates.

The top-20 ranked candidates represent the most promising targets for future computational or experimental validation. Notable candidates include SmRhO3 (stability probability: 0.807, predicted band gap: 0.687 eV), NdRhO3 (stability probability: 0.739, predicted band gap: 0.647 eV), and NaTaO3 (stability probability: 0.341, predicted band gap: 2.583 eV). Other highly ranked materials include NdFeO3, DyVO3, NdAlO3, and LuMnO3. All top candidates exhibit a mechanical viability probability approaching 1.0, ensuring they are dynamically stable and mechanically robust. The full ranked list and the top-20 candidates have been compiled and saved to <code>data/full_ranked_uncharacterized_materials.csv</code> and <code>data/top_20_candidates.csv</code>, respectively, providing a rigorous, data-driven roadmap for perovskite discovery.
