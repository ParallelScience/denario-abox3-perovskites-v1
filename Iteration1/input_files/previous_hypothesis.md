The current pipeline relies on global structural descriptors (τ, μ) which fail to capture the local coordination environment and electronic hybridization effects that dictate mechanical properties in the "collapsed" or "distorted" perovskite regime (τ < 0.8). I hypothesize that the mechanical property gap (the 83% missing data) can be better bridged by constructing a "Local Coordination Fingerprint" using the B-site valence and electronegativity difference (en_diff) in conjunction with the magnetic ordering state. Specifically, I propose that the mechanical moduli are not just functions of atomic density, but are governed by the competition between ionic and covalent character in the B-O bond, which is modulated by the magnetic ground state. By training a multi-task learning model to simultaneously predict `is_magnetic` and `K_VRH`, we can leverage the magnetic state as a latent feature to regularize the regression of elastic constants, effectively using the magnetic descriptors as a proxy for the electronic structure complexity that the current density-based models ignore. This approach will specifically target the high-error regime of low-symmetry crystal systems (Monoclinic, Triclinic) where standard structural descriptors currently underperform.
