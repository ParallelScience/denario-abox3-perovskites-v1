The current research has successfully decoupled thermodynamic stability and mechanical properties using global descriptors, but it has largely ignored the role of local coordination environments and the specific nature of the B-site transition metal d-orbital filling. I hypothesize that the "mechanical robustness" of ABO3 perovskites is not merely a function of volume and tolerance factors, but is fundamentally constrained by the B-site electronic configuration (specifically the d-electron count and spin state). By constructing a "Crystal Field-Elasticity" feature set—incorporating B-site valence, d-electron count, and magnetic moment per site—we can explain the residual variance in the 215-sample elastic dataset that current volume-based models fail to capture. Specifically, I propose that perovskites with partially filled d-orbitals (e.g., d3, d5) exhibit distinct elastic anisotropy signatures due to Jahn-Teller distortions or spin-orbit coupling, which act as "mechanical switches." By training a multi-task model that simultaneously predicts `elastic_anisotropy` and `G_VRH` using these electronic descriptors, we can identify a subset of "mechanically tunable" perovskites that are thermodynamically metastable but possess high shear rigidity, a property often overlooked in stability-first screening pipelines.
